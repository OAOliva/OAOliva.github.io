Wall Squat Machine

This was my senior capstone project which was completed with four other students in the class. We were tasked with designing and building a wall squat machine for lower body injury rehabilitation. We set a plan for ourselves and documented every stage of the design process. We met with our sponsor every week to give updates on where we were at and adjust our design to meet the requirements and specifications that we came up with together. We built an assembly in SolidWorks and machined the custom parts to match our design. We assembled the squat machine and made last minute adjustments when we found out that a part we ordered did not work as we thought it would. Our final design implemented a hydraulic system to reduce the load that the patient would have to apply themselves while completing a squat with full range of motion. A harness would support the weight of the patient during the squat. It was adjustable for patients of different sizes to comfortably use. Moving components of the hydraulic system were concealed in the back for safety and to be aesthetically pleasing. Full squat depth and range of motion of the knee was possible for patients ranging from 4' to 6'2" in height, and could hold patients that weighed up to 300 lbs.

Automatic Watering System

Designed an automated watering system for decorative plants in my house that measures moisture levels in the soil and pumps a preset amount of water if the moisture level drops below a specified number. This was done using an Arduino Nano connected to a battery, two water sensors, and a water pump. To deliver the required voltage to the water pump, which the Arduino does not output, the water pump was connected directly to the battery with a transistor functioning as an on/off switch. A small case was designed in SolidWorks to contain the Arduino and other electronic components (other than the pump and moisture sensors) and 3D printed. A tube was also 3D printed which circled the plant to evenly disperse the water. Once the system was set up, the only maintenance that was needed was refilling a large bucket of water and replacing the battery once it was depleted.

Fabric Electrode Garment - ZOLL

In this project, my mentor and I worked side by side to develop a prototype LifeVest garment and electrode belt that swapped out the existing ECGs with a conductive fabric pressed against the patient’s skin to read their heartbeat. I made a small rubber disk that the conductive fabric would wrap around to hold the same shape as the ECG pucks that were already used. I connected the conductive fabric to the existing circuit boards that did all the signal processing, noise reduction, and amplification. The first iteration could successfully read the ECG signal of the user, but a lot of noise was introduced during, especially during movement. We were not sure exactly where the noise was coming from so I started out by developing a test to compare different conductive fabrics and how well they transferred the signal, and how well they would hold up after repeated laundering. I also worked to make the connection from the ECG pucks to the circuit board as rigid as possible to reduce the movement and friction seen in the wires. I sewed the garments that we used so that they could hold the new electrode belt, and sewed the wires down so they would not move around. In parallel to reducing noise we were picking up, I used SolidWorks to model and 3-D print housings for circuit boards. I found a different way to ground the signal instead of using the existing conductive mesh that was very expensive to buy. We went through several different iterations which I quickly put together and tested, and by the end of my time there, the prototype that we made was cheaper, and more comfortable than the original garment and under specific test conditions, we did not sacrifice signal quality.

Electrode Placement Study - ZOLL

This project looked at different placements that we could move the electrodes on the LifeVest to increase ECG amplitude which allows for better identification of the user’s heartrate. My mentor and I wrote a test protocol and recruited members of the R&D team to participate in the study. To accurately measure the participants’ body and consistently place the electrodes in the correct position for each person, we scanned each participant with a 3D scanner. We used python to take a slice of the person around where their heart would be and measure the person’s circumference and give us measurements of where to place the electrodes for each test. There were four total electrodes that we placed 90 degrees apart from each other around the body. These four electrodes could make six different vectors across the body that we could measure the ECG signal through. We moved the four electrodes around the body to five different locations including the normal placement. We used python to create a heat map and rank the amplitude of the ECG signal and found that over 10 different vectors that we analyzed had higher amplitude than the normal placement. The heatmap also revealed that better placements may still exist that we did not test for, and further testing should be conducted to find an optimal placement for the electrodes.

FTIR - HMBL

I am currently working on this project where we are using frustrated total internal reflection (FTIR) technology to measure how worn-down shoe tread is. The refractive index between acrylic and air is such that light will not escape the medium that we have set up, and instead will continue to reflect within the medium. When someone steps on the acrylic plate of the device, the refractive index changes and light can escape, but only where the shoe and the acrylic are touching and there is no air in between them. I set up a Raspberry Pi and a small camera to take a picture of the shoe tread that is lit up. I then wrote a script in python that takes a picture and saves it when the user presses a button. I also used MATLAB to do all the image processing that tells us what part of the picture is lit up, the parts in contact with the shoe. This script also gives us the area of the largest illuminated continuous section of the shoe. This area can tell us how worn down the shoe tread is and if the user is at risk of slipping and falling because of how worn their shoe is. We are planning on submitting this work and presenting it at a conference in the coming months.

Ladder Rung Friction Testing - HMBL

This was the first project that I had the opportunity to work on while I was at HMBL in my sophomore year of college. I was tasked with developing a testing procedure and building the necessary equipment to measure the coefficient of friction in ladder rungs. The machine that we were using, shown in the picture, would slide a shoe across the ladder rung, measure the normal force and force it would take to pull the shoe across the surface of the ladder, and calculate the coefficient of friction. I used the machine shop in the lab to build the fixture that the attached the shoe to the machine, and the fixture that would attach the ladder rung to the base of the machine while allowing for ladder rungs to be changed quickly. The ladder rungs were also tested at different angles, so the fixture that attached the shoe to the device had to tilt the shoe to a different angle for this test. I then wrote the procedure that was used to test the ladder rungs under different conditions: dry, and contaminated with oil. This test had to be repeatable to test multiple ladder rungs with different people while still producing the same results.
