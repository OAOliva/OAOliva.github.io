Human Movement and Balance Laboratory (HMBL)

I began working at the Human Movement and Balance Laboratory (HMBL) during the fall of my sophomore year and have returned every semester since. Balancing approximately 10 hours per week between my academic responsibilities and laboratory work, I have gained extensive hands-on experience in research and engineering applications. My projects often involved uncharted territory, requiring me to adapt and broaden my skill set to bring brand new ideas into fruition.
In my first project, which focused on testing the coefficient of friction of ladder rungs, I developed a comprehensive test procedure that was used by other lab members to reliably collect data. I then used MATLAB to analyze and visualize the collected data. Additionally, I enhanced my machine shop skills by building test fixtures for the ladder rungs and shoe components.
In my most recent project, I utilized Frustrated Total Internal Reflection (FTIR) technology to research wear regions on shoes. I wrote a MATLAB script to process images by dewarping them, identifying shoe contact regions, separating continuous wear regions, and calculating the area of the largest continuous worn region. Currently, I am working on correlating the image brightness from the FTIR device to the pressure exerted by the shoe.

Projects

Wall Squat Machine

For my senior capstone project, I collaborated with a team of four peers to design and build a wall squat machine for rehabilitation, aimed at improving muscle strength and restoring range of motion in the knee for individuals recovering from lower body injuries. The machine assists users who are unable to perform bodyweight squats by applying an upward force to support the movement. The team showcased the project at the senior design expo, where we won the People's Choice Award.
Our design process began with modeling the system in SolidWorks, including stress analysis on load-bearing components to ensure structural integrity. Detailed technical drawings were created for each part, facilitating the manufacturing phase. The machine was built using standard components which we ordered online and then machined to meet our specifications. Key design features included a sliding backrest equipped with armrests for user stability and a harness system to support the user's weight.
The upward assistance force was delivered via a hydraulic cylinder and pump assembly mounted at the rear of the machine, which is attached to the backrest. To accommodate a range of users, the armrests, harness, and squat depth were fully adjustable, with the machine supporting individuals weighing up to 350 pounds and up to 6'2" in height.

Automated Watering System

The automated plant watering system is a solution to overwatering and underwatering, both of which negatively affect plant health. To mitigate these issues, I designed and implemented a fully automated watering system capable of monitoring soil moisture levels and delivering precise amounts of water when needed. The system utilizes an Arduino Nano powered by a battery and integrates two soil moisture sensors and a water pump to maintain optimal hydration levels. To overcome the Arduino's limited voltage output, the water pump was connected directly to the battery via a transistor functioning as an electronic switch. I designed and 3D-printed a custom enclosure in SolidWorks to house the Arduino and associated electronic components. Additionally, I 3D-printed a circular distribution tube to ensure even water dispersion around the plant. The system required minimal maintenance, with users only needing to periodically refill a large water reservoir and replace the battery upon depletion.

Balsa Wood Bridge

This project was completed as the final assignment for Statics 1. It was designed to deepen our understanding of the stresses experienced by individual members of a truss bridge. Collaborating with a team of three other students, we engineered a balsa wood bridge optimized to support the maximum possible weight. Our process began with using MATLAB to calculate the stresses in each bridge member and refine the design by adding additional members and plotting the maximum stress distribution. Next, we created a detailed model of the bridge in SolidWorks and performed finite element analysis (FEA) to validate our calculated stresses and optimize the design further. Finally, we constructed the bridge using balsa wood and tested it by adding weight until failure.
